## 1. PERSUASION PSYCHOLOGY

### 1.1 Robert Cialdini — The 7 Principles of Influence

**Bio:** Social psychologist at Arizona State University. Spent 3 years undercover with sales teams before publishing *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984). Added 7th principle (Unity) in *Pre-Suasion* (2016). Runs Influence at Work consulting firm.

**URLs:** influenceatwork.com | cialdini.com

**The 7 Principles (with agency/consulting applications):**

| Principle | Definition | Agency/Consulting Application |
|-----------|-----------|------------------------------|
| **Reciprocity** | People feel obliged to return favors | Give away genuinely useful frameworks, audits, or diagnostics for free. The small debt the brain wants to settle = taking the next step |
| **Commitment & Consistency** | People align with past commitments | Start with micro-yes: "Can I send you a quick assessment?" → "Here are the findings, want to discuss?" → Small yeses build to big yeses |
| **Social Proof** | People follow similar others | Hierarchy: Peer case studies > Industry testimonials > Data/statistics > Logo walls. Show "companies like yours" getting results |
| **Authority** | People follow experts | Credential the message: third-party recognition, certifications, media mentions, book authorship. Display credentials prominently |
| **Liking** | People say yes to those they like | Similarity, compliments, familiarity. Use "we" language, show genuine understanding of their specific context. Be warmly professional |
| **Scarcity** | Limited things seem more valuable | Real scarcity only: "We only take 3 clients per quarter because we do deep work." B2B buyers smell fake urgency |
| **Unity** | Shared identity influences | "We're both founders building something that matters." Identity-based language outperforms behavior-based framing |

**Key insight for B2B:** Coaches, consultants, and service providers mishandle Cialdini in two directions — either purely informative (emotionally inert) or purely tactical (fake countdown timers). The sweet spot: ethically applied principles that communicate *what you actually do* in a form the buyer's brain can evaluate.

**Real example — NHS appointment no-shows:** Cialdini reduced no-shows by 31.4% with signs saying "95% of patients at this practice turn up to appointments." Combined with asking patients to verbally repeat the appointment time (commitment + consistency).

### 1.2 Nir Eyal — Hooked Model

**Bio:** Author of *Hooked: How to Build Habit-Forming Products* (2014). Stanford Graduate School of Business lecturer. Also wrote *Indistractable*.

**URL:** nirandfar.com

**The Hook Model (4-step cycle):**

1. **Trigger** — External (notification, email, ad) → Internal (emotion, routine, boredom)
2. **Action** — Simplest behavior in anticipation of reward (BJ Fogg: B=MAT)
3. **Variable Reward** — Three types:
   - *Rewards of the Tribe* (social validation: likes, comments, shares)
   - *Rewards of the Hunt* (information/resources: feed scroll, search results)
   - *Rewards of the Self* (mastery: streaks, progress, completion)
4. **Investment** — User puts in time/data/effort/social capital → loads next trigger, increases switching costs

**Agency application:** Don't just sell a service — design a "hook loop" for your client engagement. Initial free trigger (content piece), simple action (book a call), variable reward (specific insight they didn't expect), investment (they share their situation, loading the next trigger for follow-up).

### 1.3 Susan Weinschenk — 100 Things Every Designer Needs to Know About People

**Bio:** PhD in Psychology. Chief Behavioral Scientist at The Team W. 30+ years applying behavioral science to design. Known as "The Brain Lady."

**URLs:** teamw.com | thebrainlady.com

**Key principles relevant to marketing:**
- Vision trumps all senses — half of brain's resources dedicated to seeing
- People process only 3-4 chunks of info at a time
- Stories and anecdotes persuade more than data alone
- Most decisions are made unconsciously
- If people can't feel, they can't decide
- People are happier when they're busy
- People value a product more when it's physically in front of them

**Key insight:** People can only remember/process 3-4 things. Don't give 10 choices — 3-4 maximizes satisfaction. Priming and framing work at unconscious level.

### 1.4 B2B/ Agency-Specific Persuasion Techniques

**Loss Aversion (Kahneman & Tversky):**
- Losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains feel good
- **Application:** Frame your pitch around what they lose by NOT acting (revenue leakage, competitive disadvantage, inefficiency)
- Anchor the cost of inaction BEFORE presenting your solution price
- In B2B, buyers fear career risk more than they desire gains

**Anchoring Effect in Pricing/Scoping:**
- The first number seen becomes the reference point
- **Application:** Lead with premium tier, establish high-value anchor (cost of problem), then present your price. $80K solution feels cheap after you've anchored $500K/year problem
- Presenting mid-tier after premium makes it feel reasonable

**Social Proof Hierarchy (B2B-specific):**
```
STRONGEST: Peer case study (same industry, size, role)
  → Named testimonial with results
  → Industry-specific case study
  → Third-party validation (Gartner, Forrester)
  → Customer logo wall
  WEAKEST: Generic "trusted by" badges
```

**Scarcity + Urgency That Works for B2B:**
- Fake "limited time" destroys trust (B2B buyers are sophisticated)
- **Real scarcity:** Capacity limits ("we only take 3 clients this quarter")
- **Real urgency:** Cohort starts, price increase coming, seasonal relevance
- Worst: countdown timers. Best: "We're allocating 2 months for deep work"

**Authority Transfer:**
- Position as expert through: book authorship, podcast appearances, keynote speaking, cited in major publications
- Third-party credentials (certifications, media logos) are more persuasive than self-claims
- Display credentials physically (Cialdini's hospital study: 30% higher compliance when diplomas were visible on wall)

**Commitment + Consistency (Micro-Yes Chain):**
```
Micro-yes 1: "Can I send you a free diagnostic framework?" → Yes
Micro-yes 2: "Would you be open to a 15-min debrief of your results?" → Yes
Micro-yes 3: "Based on what I'm seeing, I think I can help. Want me to put together a proposal?" → Yes
Commitment escalates naturally. Each yes makes the next yes more likely.
```

## 2. INTERRUPT PATTERNS IN MARKETING

### 2.1 Russell Brunson — Pattern Interrupts in Funnels

**Bio:** Co-founder of ClickFunnels. Author of *DotCom Secrets*, *Expert Secrets*, *Traffic Secrets*. Built billion-dollar software company.

**URL:** russellbrunson.com

**Framework: Hook → Story → Offer**
- **Hook:** Image/headline that stops the scroll — pattern interrupt
- **Story:** Builds emotional connection, epiphany bridge (leads them to their own conclusion)
- **Offer:** The solution, presented when they're ready

**Pattern Interrupt Mechanics:**
- When everyone does PowerPoint VSLs, do handwritten/hand-drawn video
- When everyone uses meme videos, use something different
- The interrupt can be as simple as a headline, color tweak, video style
- **Core insight:** Marketing is pattern recognition + pattern interruption. When a pattern becomes common, it stops working. The marketer's job: identify the current pattern, do the opposite.

**Real example:** Brunson was near bankruptcy. He hired a hand-drawn video creator (nobody was doing it). That funnel paid off the IRS and became his "one funnel away" story.

### 2.2 Gary Halbert — Opening Hooks

**Bio:** Widely considered the greatest copywriter of all time. His "Coat of Arms" letter mailed to 600M+ people over 30 years. Known for the "Dollar Bill" letter (attached a real $1 bill).

**URL:** thegaryhalbertletter.com

**The "Halbert Opener" Formula:**
```
"If you [qualifier/call-out], this is going to be the most [exciting/important] message you will ever read."
```

**9 examples:**
- "If you are already a professional model or, if you'd like to be, this is going to be the most exciting message you will ever read."
- "If you would like to make a lot of money very quickly, this is going to be the most exciting message you will ever read."
- "If you own a home in Florida, this will be the most important message you will ever read."

**Mechanics:** IF (call-out qualifier) → THEN (benefit). The reader self-identifies. Those who match *want* the benefit.

**Halbert's #1 advantage (his own words):** A powerful mailing list. Copywriting was secondary to having the right audience.

**Physical Pattern Interrupts:** Dollar bill taped to letter. Plastic bag of sand for beachfront condo pitch. Aspirin (because "first part will give you a headache"). Live postage stamp on plain envelope to look personal (A-Pile vs B-Pile).

### 2.3 The "4 U's" Headline Formula (Michael Masterson)

**Origin:** Copywriter/success coach Michael Masterson.

**Formula:**
| Element | Question | Priority for Headlines |
|---------|----------|----------------------|
| **Useful** | How does it help the reader? | #1 (non-negotiable) |
| **Urgent** | Why act now? | #2 |
| **Unique** | What's unexpected/different? | #3 |
| **Ultra-specific** | What details/numbers? | #4 |

**Scoring:** Rate each 1-4. Aim for 3+ on at least 3 of 4.

**Example transformation:**
- Weak: "Save money on your energy bill"
- Strong: "Cut your heating costs by 47% this winter with 3 insulation tweaks (before prices jump 15% in November)"

### 2.4 Formula for Interrupt Hooks (Compiled)

**1. The Surprise Opener:**
- Start with something the reader doesn't expect
- "I just deleted my entire email list. Here's why."

**2. The Broken Pattern:**
- Break a familiar format
- Instead of "How to grow your business," lead with "Why your growth is stuck (and the one question you're not asking)"

**3. The Contrarian Take:**
- Go against conventional wisdom
- "Email marketing is dead. Here's what's actually working."

**4. The Specific Before/After:**
- Show precise transformation
- "From $47K to $420K in 6 months: the exact framework we used"

**5. The "Most people think X, but actually Y":**
- Create curiosity gap by challenging common belief
- "Most people think you need more traffic. Actually, you need a better offer."

### 2.5 YouTube Thumbnail/Title Interrupt Patterns (MrBeast)

**Bio:** Jimmy Donaldson, 320M+ YouTube subscribers. Obsessive about thumbnails — $10K per thumbnail, 20+ variations per video, dedicated 6-7 person thumbnail team.

**The 5 MrBeast Thumbnail Elements:**
1. **Extreme facial expression** — shock, disbelief, wide-open mouth
2. **Visual stake** — prop communicating the premise instantly (cash, massive object)
3. **Bold numbers/money signals** — "$1,000,000," "$10,000" — huge font
4. **High contrast/saturation** — pops in feed of muted colors
5. **Text: 5 words or fewer** — amplifies, doesn't explain. Never a sentence.

**Title patterns:**
- Numerical precision ("Survive 100 Days In Circle, Win $500,000")
- Extreme contrast ("$1 vs. $1,000,000 Hotel Room!")
- Clear promise that delivers on thumbnail's visual hook

**Hook structure in videos:**
1. **Premise** — Goal of video, confirms the click
2. **Stakes** — What's on the line, creates tension
3. **Twist** — Unique angle, how it's different

**Key insight:** Thumbnail and title are designed BEFORE the video is shot. They are the product. The video delivers on their promise.

### 2.6 Email Subject Line Interrupt Patterns

- **Curiosity gap:** "Your [specific thing] isn't working (and why that's good news)"
- **Specific number + result:** "How I doubled my agency's revenue in 90 days"
- **Contrarian:** "Stop optimizing your website"
- **Personal + unexpected:** "[Name], I think you're making a mistake"
- **Pattern break:** "Are you sitting down?"
- **Useful + urgent:** "5 spots left for [specific outcome] starting Monday"

## 3. OFFER CREATION

### 3.1 Alex Hormozi — $100M Offers / Grand Slam Offer Framework

**Bio:** Owns Acquisition.com (portfolio doing $200M+/yr). Built gym chain from $0 to $100M. Author of *$100M Offers*, *$100M Lead*, *$100M Secrets*.

**URL:** acquisition.com | alexhormozi.com

**Definition:** A Grand Slam Offer = "An offer so good people feel stupid saying no." Cannot be compared to anything else in marketplace. Compete on value, not price.

**The Value Equation:**
```
Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
--------------------------------------------------- = Perceived Value
        Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice
```

**To maximize value:**
- Increase Dream Outcome (what they actually want)
- Increase Perceived Likelihood (proof, guarantees, track record)
- Decrease Time Delay (faster results = more valuable)
- Decrease Effort & Sacrifice (more you do for them = higher value)

**5-Step Grand Slam Offer Process:**

| Step | Action | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **1** | Identify Dream Outcome | What does customer really want? (Sell destination, not vehicle) |
| **2** | List ALL Problems | Exhaustive list of obstacles, fears, frictions at each step |
| **3** | Solutions List | Convert each problem into a solution ("How to [solve X]") |
| **4** | Delivery Vehicles | How to deliver: DIY, DWY (done-with-you), DFY (done-for-you) |
| **5a** | Trim | Remove low-value/high-cost. Keep: low-cost+high-value AND high-cost+high-value |
| **5b** | Stack | Bundle remaining into ultimate high-value deliverable |

**Enhancing the Offer:**
- **Scarcity:** Real capacity limits (cohort, capped seats)
- **Urgency:** Real deadlines (pricing increase, enrollment closing)
- **Bonuses:** Unbundle into named bonuses with stated value. Stack so perceived value >> price
- **Guarantees:** Single biggest conversion lever. Reverse risk. Types: unconditional, conditional, anti-guarantee. Bold guarantee signals confidence
- **Naming:** Package it as a product with a name that carries the promise

**Pricing Principle:** "Charge 8x the price for 10x the value." Stack perceived value to $4,351, charge $59. The value-to-price gap is the moat.

**Example (weight loss):**
- Dream outcome: Lose 20 lbs in 6 weeks
- Problems: don't know what to eat, no time to cook, lose motivation, bad habits
- Solutions: meal plan, short workouts, daily accountability texts, habit tracker
- Delivery: App + weekly coaching call + private community
- Stack: Named components each with perceived value ($500 meal plan + $300 workout library + $1,000 coaching = $1,800 value, sell for $197)

## 4. POSITIONING / MESSAGE STRATEGY

### 4.1 Christopher Lochhead — Category Design

**Bio:** 3x public tech CMO. 14-time #1 bestselling author (*Play Bigger*, *Category Pirates*, *Niche Down*). Advisor to 50+ VC-backed startups. Adobe named *Play Bigger* one of "5 greatest marketing books of all time."

**URL:** lochhead.com

**Core framework: Frame It → Name It → Claim It**

| Step | Action | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **Frame It** | Define the problem in a new way | Teach the world to see what's been invisible |
| **Name It** | Coin the category language | Create phrase people can't un-hear |
| **Claim It** | Own the position | Take the high ground while everyone else fights in the valley |

**The Magic Triangle:** Product Design × Company Design × Category Design. Most companies only focus on product. Legendary companies design all three.

**Category Design Scorecard (5 indicators):**
1. Frame a powerful problem
2. Articulate a noble cause/giant problem
3. Reimagined future without compromise
4. Superconsumer-driven flywheel
5. Outcome that drives massive word-of-mouth

**The Better Trap:** Don't try to be "better" than competitors in an existing category. You'll always be compared to the category king. Design a NEW category where you're the only option.

**Key concepts:**
- **Languaging:** Strategic use of language to change thinking. Listen to the words.
- **Damming the Demand:** Harness existing demand and redirect it to your new category
- **Lightning Strike:** Concentrated burst of attention that tips word-of-mouth
- **POV (Point of View):** Declare what you believe. Great categories start as acts of rebellion
- **Category > Brand:** "The category makes the brand, not the other way around"

**Real examples:**
- Salesforce created "cloud computing" / "SaaS" category
- Peloton didn't make better bikes, made "connected fitness"
- Qualtrics created "experience management" category
- Palo Alto Networks created "next-generation firewall" category

### 4.2 Geoffrey Moore — Crossing the Chasm

**Bio:** Silicon Valley marketing consultant, author of *Crossing the Chasm* (1991, sold 1M+ copies), *Zone to Win*, *The Infinite Staircase*.

**URL:** geoffreyamoore.com

**The Technology Adoption Lifecycle:**
```
Innovators (2.5%) → Early Adopters (13.5%) → [CHASM] → Early Majority (34%) → Late Majority (34%) → Laggards (16%)
```

**The Chasm:** Gap between Early Adopters (buy on vision, tolerate incomplete products) and Early Majority (buy on evidence, need proven references). Most tech companies die here.

**D-Day Strategy for Crossing the Chasm:**
1. **Target a beachhead** — single niche you can dominate completely
2. **Assemble the Whole Product** — everything the mainstream needs (not just core tech)
3. **Create the compelling reason to buy** — specific, acute pain point
4. **Position against a competitor** — not "innovative," but "better than [existing solution]"

**Positioning Statement Formula (for chasm-crossing):**
> "For [target beachhead customers] who are dissatisfied with [current alternative], our product is a [new category] that provides [key problem-solving capability]."

**Key insight:** Early market positions around the technology ("what's new and different"). Mainstream positions around the customer's pain point and reference category ("what works and is safe").

### 4.3 April Dunford — Obviously Awesome / 10-Step Positioning

**Bio:** Positioning consultant who repositioned dozens of B2B tech companies. VP Marketing at 7 startups (5 acquired, 2 IPOs).

**URL:** aprildunford.com

**The 5 Components of Positioning:**
| Component | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| Competitive Alternatives | What customers would use if you didn't exist |
| Unique Attributes | Features/capabilities you have that alternatives lack |
| Value (themes) | What those attributes enable for the customer |
| Target Customer | Who cares most about that value |
| Market Category | Context that makes your value obvious |

**The 10-Step Positioning Process (original):**
1. Understand customers who love your product
2. Form a cross-functional positioning team
3. Align your vocabulary / release "positioning baggage"
4. List your TRUE competitive alternatives (including doing nothing)
5. Isolate your unique attributes
6. Map attributes to value themes
7. Determine who cares a lot
8. Find a market frame of reference that puts your strengths at center
9. Layer on a trend (optional — don't force it)
10. Capture your positioning so it can be shared

**Three Positioning Styles:**
- **Big Fish, Small Pond:** Dominate a subsegment of existing market
- **Create a New Game:** Category creation (riskier, higher reward)
- **Same Game, Better Angle:** Enter existing market with differentiated POV

**Key insight:** Start from competitive alternatives, not your vision. Positioning exists in the customer's mind relative to what else they could do.

### 4.4 Al Ries & Jack Trout — Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind

**Bio:** Al Ries founded Ries & Ries. Jack Trout chaired Trout & Partners. Coined "positioning" in 1972 Advertising Age series. Book sold 4M+ copies in 22 languages.

**URL:** alries.com

**Core premise:** "Positioning is not what you do to a product. Positioning is what you do to the mind of the prospect."

**The Overcommunicated Society:** People are bombarded with 5,000+ messages daily. The mind filters aggressively. Only simple, clear, singular ideas survive.

**Mental Product Ladders:** Customers organize brands in mental categories on ladders. First place is powerful, second is viable, third+ is nearly irrelevant. If you can't be first, create a new ladder (new category).

**Key strategies:**
- **Being First:** The easiest way into the mind. First brand = dominant position
- **The "Against" Position:** If you can't be first in a category, position AGAINST the leader. Avis: "We're #2, so we try harder"
- **Repositioning the Competition:** Change perception of the leader's weakness. Tylenol repositioned aspirin as harsh on stomach
- **Name:** The most important marketing decision. A powerful name is simple, memorable, suggests the category position
- **Line Extension Trap:** Slapping a famous brand name on unrelated products dilutes the core position. If Volvo made sports cars, "safest car" position dies

**Key insight:** Don't create something new and different — manipulate what's already up there in the mind. Retie existing connections.

### 4.5 Harry Dry — Marketing Examples / Tactical Positioning

**Bio:** Creator of Marketing Examples newsletter (130K+ subscribers). Voted #1 marketing newsletter by marketers.

**URL:** marketingexamples.com

**5 Positioning Tactics (with examples):**
1. **Through contrast** — Point at status quo, pit yourself against it. Contrast burns brand into mind
2. **Through values** — Stand for something beyond product
3. **Through category creation** — Invent new category, have no competitors
4. **Through personality** — Let your authentic voice differentiate
5. **Through limitation** — Go all-in on one niche. Being easy to sum up makes you easy to choose

**Harry's 3 Copywriting Rules (per sentence):**
1. Can I VISUALIZE it? (Concrete, sensory language)
2. Can I FALSIFY it? (Can be proven true or false)
3. Can NOBODY ELSE SAY this? (Unique to your brand)

**Example (New Balance):** "Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio" — Visual, falsifiable, unique.

**Loom Case Study:** Shifted from "easy-to-use screen recorder" (feature-focused) to "remote communication sucks, and we're fixing it" (problem-focused). Result: worth more than the entire screen recorder category combined.

### 4.6 Andy Raskin — Strategic Narrative

**Bio:** Helps companies align around a strategic narrative. Works with CEOs on "the biggest story" for their business.

**URL:** andyraskin.com

**The Strategic Narrative Framework:**
1. **Name the BIG change in the world** — What's shifting that creates urgency?
2. **Identify the resulting problem** — What's broken because of this change?
3. **Declare your solution** — How you uniquely solve the new problem
4. **Show evidence** — Proof that your approach works
5. **Paint the new future** — What the world looks like if you win

**Key insight:** Don't lead with your product. Lead with the change in the world that makes your product necessary. The bigger the change story, the more urgent your offer becomes.

**Contrast with traditional positioning:**
- Traditional: "We help X do Y" (feature/customer focused)
- Strategic narrative: "The world is changing in Z way, which creates a new problem. Here's our solution" (change-focused)

## 6. REAL CASE STUDIES OF POSITIONING WORK

### 6.1 Salesforce — "End of Software" Category Creation

**Before:** CRM market owned by Siebel Systems. $1.8M for 200-person deployment. Software installed on-premise.

**Positioning shift:** "No Software" — created SaaS category. "End of Software" campaign with employees wearing buttons. Crashed Siebel conferences with anti-software stunts.

**Results:** Created the SaaS category. Grew to $30B+ revenue. Acquired Slack ($27.7B), Tableau ($15.7B), MuleSoft ($6.5B).

**Key tactic:** Picked a fight with Siebel to create binary choice — old world vs. new world.

### 6.2 Slack — Bottom-Up Category Domination

**Before:** Workplace chat was unproven. Email dominated. No category existed for paid team communication.

**Positioning shift:** "Email replacement for teams" → later "The nervous system of modern work." Bottom-up: individual teams adopted free product, pulled it into organizations.

**Key metrics:** 2,000 messages = 93% conversion to paid. $0 to $1B ARR in 5 years (fastest SaaS ever at the time). 12M daily active users. Acquired by Salesforce for $27.7B.

**The 2,000-message activation metric:** Teams exchanging 2,000 messages had 93% conversion rate. Every product decision oriented around getting teams to that threshold.

**Slack's positioning evolution:**
- Uncharted Waters (2013-15): Category pioneer, "beautiful chat"
- First Voyage (2015-17): "Email killer," 1M DAU
- Charted Course (2017-19): Category is real, Microsoft enters
- Crowded Waters (2020-21): Must bulk up → sells to Salesforce

### 6.3 Loom — Category Reframe

**Before:** "Easy-to-use screen recorder" — competing in existing screen recorder bucket.

**After:** "Remote communication sucks, and we're fixing it" — problem-focused category.

**Result:** Worth more than the entire screen recorder bucket combined. Category leadership and increased valuation.

**Framework used (per Harry Dry):**
- Old: Feature-focused ("we're an easy to use screen recorder")
- New: Problem-focused ("no one likes long Zoom calls, emails are too impersonal")

### 6.4 HubSpot — Inbound Marketing Category

**Before:** Marketing software was about outbound, interruption-based marketing.

**Positioning shift:** Created "Inbound Marketing" category. "The inbound marketing platform." Wrote books on inbound. Ran conferences on inbound. Category IS the product.

**Result:** Created a billion-dollar category. Competitors forced to play on their turf.

## 8. KEY FRAMEWORKS SUMMARY

### The Ultimate Agency/Consulting Persuasion Stack

```
1. ATTRACT (Interrupt Patterns)
   → Hook: Contrarian/surprise/pattern-break opener
   → Subject line: Curiosity gap, specific result
   → Visual: High contrast, face with emotion, clear stake

2. ENGAGE (Psychology)
   → Reciprocity: Free diagnostic/audit
   → Authority: Third-party credentials, results
   → Liking: Specific understanding of THEIR world
   → Social proof: "Companies like yours" case studies

3. COMMIT (Micro-Yes Chain)
   → Free content → Small ask → Deeper conversation → Proposal
   → Each yes makes next yes more likely
   → Commitment + Consistency principle

4. OFFER (Grand Slam Structure)
   → Dream Outcome × Likelihood / Time × Effort
   → Stack value: 8x perceived, 1x price
   → Risk reversal (guarantee)
   → Real scarcity (capacity, cohort)

5. CLOSE (Loss Aversion + Anchoring)
   → Anchor cost of inaction FIRST
   → Frame around what they lose by not acting
   → Present solution as loss prevention
   → Social proof that others survived this decision
```
